# AssayShift-TF: Protocol-Aware Benchmarking for Transcription Factor Binding Prediction Under Distribution Shift

**Motivation.** Transcription factor (TF) binding predictors are often evaluated with random or near-random genomic splits that reward interpolation across shared assays, laboratories, species, and TF families. In practice, models are used in less forgiving settings: a new lab measures the same factor with a different protocol, a model trained on human data is applied to mouse, or a predictor is asked to score a TF family with sparse labeled examples. These deployment axes are scientifically meaningful because TF binding labels reflect both sequence grammar and experimental protocol. A benchmark that ignores protocol structure can therefore overstate biological generalization and obscure when predictions are calibrated enough to trust.

**Problem.** We study TF-binding prediction under structured distribution shift. Given sequence-centered genomic examples, binding labels, and metadata describing assay type, laboratory or data source, species, TF identity, TF family, and cellular context when available, the goal is to estimate binding probabilities that remain discriminative and calibrated under held-out protocol, lab, species, and TF-family shifts.

**Contributions.**

1. **Protocol-aware benchmark.** We define AssayShift-TF, a benchmark specification that turns TF-binding datasets into explicit shift splits: in-distribution controls, held-out assay or protocol, held-out lab or source, held-out species, and held-out TF family.
2. **PICARD-TF / AxisGuard-CNN.** We introduce a protocol-factorized CNN that learns a sequence-derived binding representation while using TF/family embeddings and a guarded protocol branch. During training, assay/lab/species metadata are counterfactually masked, and a residual penalty discourages protocol shortcuts.
3. **Shift-aware model comparison.** We compare GC artifact controls, k-mer logistic regression, k-mer-plus-metadata logistic regression, a plain sequence CNN, and AxisGuard-CNN under identical splits.
4. **Calibration and abstention under shift.** Beyond AUROC and AUPRC, we evaluate expected calibration error, Brier score, worst-group performance, bootstrap confidence intervals, reliability curves, and selective prediction risk-coverage curves.
5. **Failure analysis for biology and protocol.** We organize errors by GC and sequence complexity, metadata group, TF family, and protocol axis to distinguish plausible biological signal from assay artifacts.

**Method.** AssayShift-TF represents each example as a fixed-length DNA sequence window centered on a candidate binding event, a binary binding label, and a metadata record. The benchmark supports GC-only artifact controls, k-mer logistic regression, k-mer-plus-metadata logistic regression, a plain sequence CNN, and AxisGuard-CNN. AxisGuard-CNN encodes the DNA window with a small Conv1D network, embeds TF and TF-family identity, and routes assay/lab/species/cellular metadata through a protocol branch. Its training objective combines binary cross entropy, counterfactual consistency under protocol metadata masking, a metadata residual penalty, and an optional assay/lab adversary. Fields corresponding to the held-out split axis and direct correlates are masked to reduce split-label leakage. The report generator emits per-split metrics, bootstrap confidence intervals, reliability plots, selective prediction curves, and grouped failure tables.

**Evaluation Plan.** We will construct splits along five axes. The random or chromosome-held-out split estimates ordinary within-protocol performance. The assay or protocol split holds out one measurement technology or processing protocol. The lab or source split holds out data from one producing group or source when metadata are available. The species split trains on one species and evaluates on another, with separate reporting for shared TFs or orthologous families where possible. The TF-family split holds out one or more TF families to test extrapolation beyond related motifs. For each split, we will report AUROC, AUPRC, precision at fixed recall, Brier score, ECE, worst-group AUPRC, and selective risk at fixed coverage levels. We will also compare calibration before and after temperature scaling or isotonic calibration fit only on in-distribution validation data.

**Observed Preliminary Result.** We ran two reproducible real-data pilots. First, a 3-TF K562 assay-shift pilot on hg19 trains on ENCODE TF ChIP-seq peaks for CTCF, MAX, and MYC and evaluates held-out assay performance on deterministic peaks called from public GSE84474 CUT&RUN fragments with a no-antibody control. Sequence windows use equal positives and GC-matched negatives. The k-mer-plus-metadata baseline reaches IID AUPRC 0.765 [0.742, 0.798] and held-out CUT&RUN AUPRC 0.715 [0.694, 0.736], while uncalibrated ECE worsens from 0.159 [0.143, 0.187] to 0.243 [0.229, 0.263]. A plain CNN improves held-out CUT&RUN AUPRC to 0.796 [0.775, 0.814] with calibrated ECE 0.044 [0.041, 0.064]. AxisGuard-CNN is competitive at full coverage, AUPRC 0.790 [0.769, 0.807] and calibrated ECE 0.074 [0.065, 0.092], and slightly improves the most selective retained subset, reaching calibrated AUPRC 0.959 at 20% coverage versus 0.952 for the plain CNN. Second, an ENCODE-only GRCh38 K562 benchmark across eight TF ChIP-seq rows tests lab and TF-family stress splits with GC+accessibility-matched negatives. Under held-out HAIB lab shift, AxisGuard-CNN improves over the plain CNN from AUPRC 0.702 [0.677, 0.735] to 0.731 [0.706, 0.766], calibrated ECE 0.092 [0.079, 0.113] to 0.032 [0.027, 0.060], and calibrated Brier score 0.209 [0.201, 0.215] to 0.196 [0.187, 0.203]. Under held-out zinc-finger family shift, the plain CNN remains strongest at full coverage, highlighting that protocol guarding improves some reliability/stress settings but is not a universal ranking win.

**Limitations.** The benchmark depends on metadata completeness and harmonization across sources. Assay, lab, cell type, genome build, peak caller, read depth, and negative sampling can be partially confounded, so split results should be interpreted as deployment stress tests rather than clean causal estimates. Cross-species evaluation may mix biological divergence with genome annotation and orthology uncertainty. TF-family splits can be small or imbalanced. Finally, calibration measured on available labels may not guarantee calibrated probabilities for all regulatory contexts, especially when negatives include unmeasured or condition-specific binding events.

**Impact.** AssayShift-TF asks a sharper question than "does the model predict TF binding?": when the protocol, source, species, or TF family changes, what still works, what becomes miscalibrated, and when should the model abstain? The current results support a cautious model claim: protocol-factorized guarding can improve calibration and lab-shift robustness, while the benchmark exposes where a plain sequence CNN still wins.
